The one‑sentence answer. Session 1 won because it changed which kernel runs (dispatch routing) — a cheap, high‑leverage decision the existing kernels already made suboptimally. Sessions 2 & 3 failed because they tried to make the kernels themselves faster, and the FlyDSL kernels are already sitting at a hardware co‑optimum (25% occupancy, barrier‑/latency‑bound) where every in‑body lever is either load‑bearing, occupancy‑locked, or structural to a schedule that already matches the vendor library on most shapes.

2 · Why Session 1 worked

The win was not a faster kernel — it was a better choice between two kernels that already existed. FlyDSL ships two forward paths on gfx950:

  • DUALWAVE_SWP — a hand‑scheduled, long‑sequence latency‑hiding pipeline (256‑row tiles, 8 waves, 2‑buffer LDS, software‑pipelined). Excellent on long / GQA / split‑K / varlen.
  • Generic M128/M256 — a simpler tiled kernel. Faster on very short sequences where the long pipeline's fixed prologue/epilogue cost dominates.

PR683 routed all dense shapes to DUALWAVE_SWP. At S=128 that pays a pipeline tax with almost no sequence to amortize it over. Session 1's gate simply sends short dense shapes to the generic kernel instead.

Why this is high‑leverage and low‑risk:
  • ✔ Both kernels already existed and were already correct — no new numerics, no new hazards. Net change touched one dispatcher file, zero kernel math.
  • ✔ The improvement was a real mistake being corrected, not new performance being invented — S=128 was being sent to the wrong tool.
  • ✔ Bounded blast radius — only short dense shapes re‑route; protected buckets (long/GQA/varlen) are byte‑identical.

Session 1's own honest caveat: the win was narrow (one shape slice) precisely because the plan's lower bound permitted a "≥1 candidate" stop, and the cheapest satisfying path is a dispatch tweak. That set up the central question for Sessions 2 & 3.

3 · Why Sessions 2 & 3 kept failing

Both sessions targeted the kernels themselves. The profiling is identical and damning across every shape and provider:

The binding constraint (measured, every provider/tile): 4 waves/CU = 25% occupancy, register‑limited (arch_vgpr ~239–256). No bucket is compute/MFMA‑bound (MFMA ≤ 7–8%). The limiters are always memory/latency: s_barrier, vmcnt (global‑load wait), lgkmcnt (LDS wait). And critically — aiter_ck wins the few gap shapes while sitting at the same 25% ceiling, so its edge is a fundamentally different instruction schedule, not more occupancy.

4 · Why so few rounds before exiting

This is the part worth internalizing for the workflow. The sessions didn't quit from impatience — they quit because the search space of cheap/medium levers is small and was exhausted fast, and the loop's control logic then spun.

  • Session 2 (~3 rounds): only a handful of in‑body levers exist on a hand‑tuned kernel, and the constraint (VGPR‑locked 25% occupancy + load‑bearing barriers) kills most of them on contact. Once occupancy (not viable), barriers (correctness), padding (wrong root cause), and prefetch‑depth (barrier‑bound) were each falsified with evidence, there was simply nothing cheap left to try. The honest result arrived quickly.
  • Session 3 (9 rounds, exited via stagnation circuit‑breaker): the technical result was equally fast — ~4 rounds produced the baseline, diagnosis, and all four falsified levers. Rounds 5–9 produced no new technical findings; they were consumed by evidence‑completeness, provenance exactness, and a governance deadlock (below).
The governance deadlock (the real reason Session 3 dragged then timed out). The plan permitted an evidence‑backed negative result as its minimum‑acceptable outcome — but the RLCR loop's COMPLETE rule required every acceptance criterion met. The must‑win speedup criterion was unreachable, so the loop could never emit a clean "done." It kept demanding "more evidence" for a conclusion that was already firm, a human had to step in to accept the negative result, and even then the loop ran on until a stagnation circuit‑breaker finally tripped at round 9. The deepest lesson is not about GPUs at all: the methodology lacked a terminating "accepted negative‑result" verdict. (Filed upstream as a methodology improvement.)

Put plainly: Session 1 picked the low‑hanging fruit; Sessions 2 & 3 found the tree was already harvested. That is itself a valuable result — the pilot produced an evidence map showing FlyDSL's gfx950 forward attention is at or beyond the vendor library across the vast majority of the shape space, with the remaining gap being structural and confined to small‑batch mid‑sequence shapes.

6 · What's actually left on the table

  • A dispatch‑only follow‑up (real, landable): the Session‑3 matrix found current‑auto routing is sub‑optimal in 24/80 cells (up to ~22%, mostly B=8 GQA, where it routes to generic but DUALWAVE_SWP is faster). This is Session 1's winning pattern again — fix the routing, not the kernel. (Excluded from Sessions 2/3 only because their must‑win bar forbade dispatch‑only wins.)
  • A from‑scratch short/mid kernel matching aiter's small‑batch schedule — the only path to the structural S=128/192/256 B=1 gap. High effort, narrow payoff; warrants its own plan if the regime ever becomes a production bottleneck.
